About Edward

St. Thomas · New York · Atlanta · Global

A life shaped by place, service and story.

Edward La Borde Jr. is a producer, director, writer and editor whose career connects Caribbean identity, independent filmmaking, public media and humanitarian storytelling.

From St. Thomas to New York, the U.S. Navy, San Francisco and Atlanta, each chapter has expanded the perspective he brings to stories about culture, community and human possibility.

The Story

Rooted in the Caribbean.
Expanded by the world.

01

Early Years

Born in St. Thomas, U.S. Virgin Islands, Edward spent formative years growing up in New York. The two places offered distinct ways of seeing the world: the cultural intimacy and visual character of the Caribbean alongside the energy, scale and range of experiences found in New York.

That combination continues to shape his work. Place is never merely a backdrop; it carries history, identity and emotional meaning.

02

United States Navy

Edward served four years in U.S. Navy Visual Communications, with assignments extending from the Far East to the Middle East during the Gulf War era. He completed his service with an honorable discharge in 1993.

The Navy became one chapter in a larger creative journey, strengthening the discipline, preparation, adaptability and responsibility he later carried into production leadership.

03

Academy of Art University

After military service, Edward studied motion picture, video and film production at Academy of Art University in San Francisco. The Bay Area placed him in an independent creative community and gave him the formal foundation to turn a lifelong interest in storytelling into a profession.

04

Afro-Flicks Productions

Edward founded Afro-Flicks Productions to create varied, authentic images of the African Diaspora. Its vision extended across African Diaspora, Caribbean and American cultures, with an emphasis on high-quality stories capable of inspiring and uplifting audiences.

The company established the creative values that still guide his work: humility, collaboration and diversity.

05

Feature Filmmaking

Edward’s independent feature work includes Me and Mrs. Jones, Get Money and Timeless: A Virgin Islands Love Story. Across those productions he has worked as a writer, director and producer, building films through long-term creative partnerships and resourceful independent production.

Me and Mrs. Jones earned Best Feature at the Hollywood Black Film Festival and reached nationwide distribution. Timeless brought Edward’s filmmaking home to the Virgin Islands and is available on Tubi.

06

Creative Leadership

Edward’s perspective comes from working across the entire storytelling process: producing, directing, writing and editing. He understands how an early idea must evolve through planning, performance, field production and post-production before it becomes a finished experience for an audience.

His leadership style is collaborative. Clear preparation creates room for cast members, community participants, clients and crews to contribute their strongest work.

07

Tourism, Public Media and Humanitarian Storytelling

From 2003 to 2006, Edward served as Director of Film Promotion and Film Commissioner for the U.S. Virgin Islands Department of Tourism, connecting filmmaking with the cultural and economic story of place.

He later produced extensively for WTJX PBS in the U.S. Virgin Islands, including feature documentaries, educational programming, live television and Carnival coverage. Today, as a Video Producer for Habitat for Humanity International, he creates documentary, branded and humanitarian stories that center communities and translate complex issues into accessible human narratives.

08

Today

Based in the Atlanta area, Edward continues to develop independent film work while producing stories with national and international reach. The settings and formats may change, but the commitment remains consistent: create work that connects people, respects culture and gives purpose a cinematic form.

Storytelling Philosophy

What matters is who the story allows us to see.

I tell stories because they can hold memory, honor people and make distance feel smaller. My Caribbean roots taught me that place and culture are never background—they are part of the character of every story.

Whether I am working with a community, an organization or a cast and crew, I want the finished work to preserve what matters, celebrate the people at its center and leave audiences moved to see or act differently.
